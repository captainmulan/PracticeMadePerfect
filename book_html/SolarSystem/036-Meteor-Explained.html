📚 Meteors Explained

Learn about shooting stars!

🪨 Three Names for Space Rocks

Scientists use three words for the same kind of object at different stages:

  • Meteoroid — A rock floating in space
  • Meteor — The bright streak when it burns in the sky
  • Meteorite — A piece that survives and lands on Earth

💡 Fun Fact

About 44 tons of space dust and tiny rocks hit Earth every day — most are too small to see!

🔥 Why They Glow

When a meteoroid slams into air at incredible speed, friction heats it until it glows. The air around it gets super hot too — that's the bright trail you see!

Most meteoroids burn up completely before reaching the ground. Only the toughest rocks become meteorites.

🌠 Meteor Showers

Comets leave trails of dust behind them as they orbit the Sun. When Earth's orbit crosses that dust trail, we get a meteor shower — dozens of meteors per hour!

Each shower is named after the constellation they seem to come from, like the Perseids (from Perseus) or Leonids (from Leo).

💥 Big Impacts

Sometimes a very large meteoroid hits Earth. About 66 million years ago, a huge impact may have helped end the age of dinosaurs!

Meteor Crater in Arizona is a famous hole made by a meteorite about 50,000 years ago. It is almost 1.2 km wide!

🔭 Watching the Sky

The best time to see meteors is on a dark, moonless night away from city lights. Lie back, let your eyes adjust, and look at the whole sky.

You don't need a telescope — meteors move too fast! Just patience and a cozy blanket.
